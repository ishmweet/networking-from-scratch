## 1. What is Packet Sniffing?

### 📘 Formal Definition

**Packet sniffing** (also called **packet capturing** or **network sniffing**) is the practice of **intercepting and recording data packets** as they travel across a network, allowing the captured traffic to be stored, analyzed, and inspected in detail — either for legitimate network analysis or malicious interception purposes.

### 🧠 Simple Explanation

Every piece of data traveling across a network — web requests, emails, login credentials, file transfers — exists briefly as a packet in transit. Packet sniffing is the act of **reaching out and grabbing those packets** as they fly past, reading their contents in the same way you might intercept and read a physical letter.

### 📮 Real-World Analogy: Intercepting Physical Mail

> Packet sniffing is like a postal worker who, instead of simply delivering each letter, **opens, reads, and photographs every envelope that passes through their hands** — then re-seals and delivers it so the recipient never knows it was read. If the letter was written in a foreign language (encryption), they can still photograph it, but the contents remain meaningless to them. If the letter was written in plain English (cleartext), every word is immediately readable.

### 🌍 Real-World Examples

- A network engineer capturing traffic to diagnose why a specific application is behaving unexpectedly
- A security analyst capturing traffic from a suspected compromised machine to analyze what data it's sending
- An attacker on a public Wi-Fi network intercepting login credentials from users on unencrypted sites
- A SOC analyst capturing and analyzing a PCAP file containing evidence of data exfiltration

## 2. Why Packet Sniffing Exists

### 🎯 Legitimate Uses
- **Network troubleshooting:** Seeing exactly what traffic is being generated to diagnose connectivity, performance, or application issues (*Chapter 22: Wireshark*)
- **Security monitoring:** Capturing traffic for analysis by IDS/IPS systems (*Chapter 30*), detecting anomalies and threats
- **Forensics and incident response:** Preserving evidence of attack traffic for investigation
- **Protocol learning:** Understanding how protocols actually behave in practice versus theory

### ⚠️ Malicious Uses
- **Credential theft:** Capturing usernames and passwords transmitted over unencrypted protocols
- **Session hijacking:** Stealing session cookies (*Chapter 14, Section 7*) to impersonate authenticated users
- **Data theft:** Intercepting sensitive business, financial, or personal data in transit
- **Reconnaissance:** Mapping a network's topology, active devices, and communication patterns

> 🔑 Packet sniffing is a **dual-use capability** — the exact same tool and technique serves both legitimate defenders and malicious attackers. Context, authorization, and intent determine whether it's a vital security tool or a criminal act.

## 3. How Packet Sniffing Works — The Core Concept

A packet sniffer places a network interface into **promiscuous mode** (*Chapter 22, Section 3*) — allowing it to capture all packets on the network segment, not just those addressed to its own MAC address.

```
 Normal NIC behavior:
 Frame for MAC: AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF (this device) -> Captured ✓
 Frame for MAC: 11:22:33:44:55:66 (other device) -> Discarded ✗

 Promiscuous mode NIC behavior:
 Frame for MAC: AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF (this device) -> Captured ✓
 Frame for MAC: 11:22:33:44:55:66 (other device) -> Captured ✓
 Broadcast frames                               -> Captured ✓
 ALL frames on the segment                      -> Captured ✓
```

The sniffer then records captured packets to memory or disk for immediate display or later analysis.

> 🔑 A packet sniffer can only capture traffic that reaches its network interface. Understanding **where** the sniffer is placed on the network is just as important as understanding **how** it captures packets.

## 4. Passive vs. Active Sniffing

### 🎯 Passive Sniffing

**Passive sniffing** involves simply **listening** to traffic on the network without sending any additional packets or manipulating the network in any way.

```
 [ Attacker (Sniffer) ]
         |
 Just listens to traffic passing by — no packets sent
```

**When it works:**
- On **hub-based networks** (*Chapter 17, Section 9*) — hubs broadcast all traffic to all ports, so every device naturally sees all traffic
- On **wireless networks** in monitor mode (*Chapter 21, Section 7*) — all wireless frames in range are receivable
- On your own network segment with broadcast traffic

**Limitation:** On modern **switched networks** (*Chapter 17*), passive sniffing only captures your own traffic plus broadcasts and multicasts — not unicast traffic between other devices.

### 🎯 Active Sniffing

**Active sniffing** involves **sending additional packets** to manipulate the network and redirect traffic toward the attacker's machine, enabling interception of traffic that wouldn't otherwise be visible.

**Common techniques:**
- **ARP Poisoning** — manipulating ARP tables to redirect traffic (*Chapter 25*)
- **MAC Flooding** — overwhelming the switch's CAM table to force hub-like behavior (*Chapter 17, Section 15*)
- **DHCP Spoofing** — acting as a rogue DHCP server to assign a malicious gateway (*Chapter 11, Section 12*)

> 🔑 Most real-world malicious sniffing on modern switched networks requires **active techniques** to overcome the natural traffic isolation that switches provide — meaning the attacker must be generating and sending packets, not just passively listening.

## 5. Sniffing on Switched Networks

### 🎯 The Challenge

As covered in *Chapter 17*, modern switches use MAC address tables (CAM tables) to forward frames **only to the intended destination port** — unlike hubs, which broadcast everything everywhere. This means a device connected to one port can't normally see unicast traffic destined for devices on other ports.

```
 Switch:
 Port 1: PC-A (Attacker)
 Port 2: PC-B (Victim)
 Port 3: Router

 PC-B sends HTTP credentials to Router ->
 Switch forwards ONLY to Port 3
 PC-A on Port 1 sees NOTHING
```

### 🎯 Overcoming This: ARP Poisoning

The most common technique for sniffing on a switched network is **ARP Poisoning** (*Chapter 25*) — where the attacker sends forged ARP replies to trick both the victim and the gateway into sending their traffic through the attacker's machine first.

```
 After ARP Poisoning:
 PC-B thinks Router's MAC is Attacker's MAC
 ->  PC-B sends all "gateway-bound" traffic to Attacker
 ->  Attacker captures it, then forwards to real Router
 ->  PC-B never notices (Man-in-the-Middle)
```

## 6. Common Packet Sniffing Tools

| Tool | Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Wireshark** | GUI, cross-platform | The most widely used packet analyzer — covered in full in *Chapter 22* |
| **tcpdump** | Command-line, Linux/macOS | Lightweight, scriptable packet capture tool using PCAP format |
| **tshark** | Command-line | The command-line version of Wireshark |
| **Ettercap** | GUI + CLI | Comprehensive MITM and sniffing tool, supports ARP poisoning |
| **Bettercap** | CLI, interactive | Modern MITM framework with sniffing, ARP poisoning, and more |
| **Dsniff** | CLI | Suite of tools for network auditing, including credential sniffing |
| **Scapy** | Python library | Programmable packet manipulation and capture framework |
| **Kismet** | CLI/GUI | Wireless packet sniffer and WIDS (Wireless IDS) |

### Choosing the Right Tool

| Goal | Recommended Tool |
|---|---|
| Interactive packet analysis | Wireshark |
| Command-line packet capture | tcpdump |
| Automated scripting | Scapy |
| Wireless reconnaissance | Kismet |
| Man-in-the-Middle testing | Bettercap or Ettercap |

> 🔑 Most professionals capture packets with **tcpdump** and perform detailed analysis later in **Wireshark**.


### 🧠 tcpdump — The Command-Line Essential

**tcpdump** is available on virtually every Linux and macOS system by default and is often the fastest way to capture traffic in a terminal:

```bash
tcpdump -i eth0                    # Capture on interface eth0
tcpdump -i eth0 port 80            # Capture only HTTP traffic
tcpdump -i eth0 host 192.168.1.10  # Capture traffic to/from this IP
tcpdump -i eth0 -w capture.pcap    # Save to PCAP file for Wireshark
tcpdump -i eth0 -r capture.pcap    # Read from existing PCAP file
```

## 7. What Can Be Captured?

The answer depends on **what protocols are in use** — specifically, whether they encrypt their data in transit.

### 🔓 Unencrypted Protocols — Fully Readable

| Protocol | Port | What's Exposed |
|---|---|---|
| **HTTP** | 80 | Full URL, request headers, response body, cookies, **credentials** |
| **FTP** | 21 | **Usernames and passwords in cleartext**, file contents |
| **Telnet** | 23 | **Every keystroke**, including **passwords**, in cleartext |
| **SMTP** | 25 | Email content, **credentials** |
| **POP3** | 110 | Email content, **credentials** |
| **DNS** | 53 (UDP) | Every domain name queried — complete browsing history |
| **SNMP v1/v2** | 161 | Community strings (effectively passwords), device information |

### 🔐 Encrypted Protocols — Content Not Readable

| Protocol | Port | What's (Still) Visible |
|---|---|---|
| **HTTPS/TLS** | 443 | Destination IP, certificate details, SNI (domain name), timing and size |
| **SSH** | 22 | That a connection occurred, source/destination, timing |
| **IMAPS/SMTPS** | 993/465 | Connection metadata only |

> 🔑 Even with encryption, **metadata** (who talked to whom, when, for how long, how much data) remains visible in packet captures. This metadata alone can be highly revealing — a topic directly relevant to privacy and intelligence work.

## 8. Capturing Credentials Over Cleartext Protocols

One of the most compelling demonstrations of why encryption matters — and one of the most dangerous capabilities of malicious sniffing — is credential capture over unencrypted protocols.

### 🧠 Example: FTP Credential Capture

```
 Captured Telnet/FTP session:
 -> USER admin
 <- 331 Password required
 -> PASS MyS3cretP@ssword
 <- 230 Login successful
```

Every character — including the password — travels in **plain readable text**. A sniffer on the same network segment captures this instantly.

### 🧠 Example: HTTP Form Submission

A login form submitted over HTTP (not HTTPS) sends credentials in the HTTP POST body, fully visible in any packet capture:

```
 POST /login HTTP/1.1
 Host: example.com

 username=admin&password=MyS3cretP@ssword
```

> 🔑 This is why **HTTPS** (*Chapter 14*) and **TLS** (*Chapter 15*) are not optional niceties — they're the essential defense against credential capture by any sniffing-capable attacker on the network path.

## 9. Sniffing Wireless Traffic

Wireless networks present a unique sniffing surface, because unlike wired networks where traffic is confined to cables, **wireless signals propagate through the air** and can be received by any device within range.

### 🎯 Monitor Mode

Setting a wireless interface to **monitor mode** (*Chapter 21, Section 7*) allows it to capture **all wireless frames** in range — including frames from networks the device is not connected to.

```bash
# Enable monitor mode (Linux):
airmon-ng start wlan0

# Capture all wireless traffic:
tcpdump -i wlan0mon

# Or use specialized wireless capture tools:
airodump-ng wlan0mon
```

### 🎯 What Can Be Seen on Wireless Networks

| Network Type | What's Capturable |
|---|---|
| **Open/Unencrypted Wi-Fi** | All traffic in cleartext — full packet content readable |
| **WEP-Encrypted** | Encrytion is broken — traffic decryptable with minimal effort |
| **WPA2-Personal** | Encrypted but vulnerable if the 4-way handshake is captured and the passphrase is weak |
| **WPA3** | Significantly harder to decrypt even with handshake capture — SAE provides forward secrecy |

## 10. How Encryption Defeats Sniffing

Encryption is the **single most effective defense** against packet sniffing of content.

```
 Without Encryption (HTTP):
 Captured packet contains:
 "GET /login HTTP/1.1
  username=admin&password=secret123"
 -> Fully readable by attacker

 With Encryption (HTTPS/TLS):
 Captured packet contains:
 "17 03 03 00 28 a1 f9 3c 7b 2e 4d 8a 1c 9f 0e..."
 -> Meaningless ciphertext — no usable information
```

Even if an attacker successfully captures every packet in an encrypted session, **they cannot read the content** without the decryption key (*Chapter 15*).

### 🧠 What Encryption Doesn't Hide

Even with strong encryption, a packet capture still reveals:
- **Source and destination IP addresses** — who is talking to whom
- **Timing and volume** — when communication occurred and how much data was transferred
- **DNS queries** (unless DoH/DoT is used — *Chapter 10, Section 11*)
- **Server Name Indication (SNI)** — the hostname in TLS ClientHello packets

## 11. Port Mirroring and Legitimate Sniffing

### 🎯 The Problem for Legitimate Monitoring

Modern switches prevent casual sniffing by only forwarding traffic to its intended port. How do legitimate monitoring tools capture traffic from across the network?

### 🎯 Port Mirroring (SPAN)

**Port mirroring** (also called **SPAN — Switched Port Analyzer**) is a switch feature that **copies traffic from one or more ports to a designated monitoring port**, allowing a packet analyzer or IDS/IPS to see traffic without being in the traffic path.

```
 [ Switch ]
   Port 1 -- PC-A (source port)
   Port 2 -- PC-B (source port)
   Port 3 -- IDS/Wireshark (mirror port — receives copies of all traffic on Port 1 and 2)
```

### 🎯 Network TAPs

A **network TAP (Test Access Point)** is a physical device installed inline on a network link that creates a **passive copy of all traffic** for monitoring purposes without interfering with the original traffic flow.

```
 [ Switch ] --- [ TAP ] --- [ Router ]
                   |
                   v
          [ Monitoring Device ]
```

> 🔑 Port mirroring and TAPs are how **legitimate IDS/IPS systems** (*Chapter 30*), **network monitoring tools**, and **SOC analysts** capture and analyze traffic at scale, without needing to resort to active sniffing techniques.

### SPAN vs. TAP

| Feature | SPAN (Port Mirroring) | Network TAP |
|---|---|---|
| Implemented in | Switch | Dedicated hardware |
| Packet Loss | Possible under heavy load | Very unlikely |
| Deployment Cost | Low | Higher |
| Common Usage | Enterprise monitoring | High-performance monitoring and forensics |

## 12. How This Works in Real Life

| Environment | Packet Sniffing in Practice |
|---|---|
| **Home Networks** | Rarely monitored, but an attacker who joins the same Wi-Fi network can perform active sniffing against unencrypted traffic |
| **Enterprise Networks** | Security-conscious enterprises deploy IDS/IPS sensors fed by port mirroring; forward proxies (*Chapter 20*) inspect web traffic; all legitimate sniffing is authorized and documented |
| **Data Centers** | Network monitoring infrastructure captures flow data and full PCAP for security analysis and troubleshooting; typically fed via TAPs or port mirroring on core switches |
| **Cloud Environments** | Full packet capture is typically limited by cloud providers; VPC flow logs provide metadata-level visibility; some providers offer specialized packet capture services |
| **SOC** | Packet captures (PCAPs) are a primary evidence source during incident response — analysts use Wireshark to examine captured traffic for indicators of compromise, data exfiltration, and C2 communication |

## 13. Packet Sniffing and Cybersecurity

| Security Context | Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Credential Theft** | Cleartext protocols expose credentials directly — sniffing on a compromised network segment immediately yields authentication material |
| **Session Hijacking** | Captured session cookies (*Chapter 14, Section 7*) can be replayed to impersonate authenticated users without knowing their passwords |
| **Reconnaissance** | Traffic analysis reveals active hosts, used protocols, DNS queries, and communication patterns — all valuable for mapping a network |
| **Man-in-the-Middle Enablement** | Sniffing is the capture phase of a MITM attack (*Chapter 27*) — ARP poisoning or DHCP spoofing redirects traffic to the attacker, who sniffs and forwards it |
| **Data Exfiltration Detection** | Defenders analyze captured traffic to detect large or unusual outbound flows indicating data theft |
| **Malware C2 Detection** | Regular beaconing patterns and unusual protocol usage visible in packet captures indicate malware activity |
| **Evidence Collection** | In incident response, PCAP files serve as forensic evidence — preserving exactly what data was transmitted during an attack |

## 14. Defending Against Malicious Sniffing

| Defense | How It Helps |
|---|---|
| **Encryption (HTTPS, TLS, SSH)** | Makes captured content unreadable even if packets are intercepted — the single most effective defense |
| **Switching (not hubs)** | Modern switches naturally limit passive sniffing to the local segment — already universal in modern networks |
| **Encrypted Wi-Fi (WPA2/WPA3)** | Encrypts wireless traffic, preventing open-air interception of content |
| **Dynamic ARP Inspection (DAI)** | Prevents ARP poisoning-based traffic redirection (*Chapter 12, Section 11*) |
| **DHCP Snooping** | Prevents rogue DHCP servers from redirecting traffic (*Chapter 11, Section 12*) |
| **802.1X Authentication** | Prevents unauthorized devices from joining the network segment in the first place (*Chapter 21, Section 11*) |
| **VPNs** | Encrypts all traffic end-to-end, protecting against interception even by an attacker already on the network (*Chapter 31*) |
| **DNS over HTTPS/TLS (DoH/DoT)** | Encrypts DNS queries, preventing DNS-based traffic analysis (*Chapter 10, Section 11*) |
| **Network Monitoring for Promiscuous Mode** | Some tools can detect NICs on a network that are in promiscuous mode — a potential indicator of sniffing activity |

## 15. Troubleshooting Perspective

| Problem | Symptom | How Packet Sniffing Helps | Common Finding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Application sending unexpected data | Unknown external connections observed in firewall logs | Capture traffic from the application's host and inspect the full packet contents | Cleartext credentials, misconfigured endpoint, or malware C2 traffic |
| Intermittent connectivity | Packets being dropped or corrupted | Capture at the affected segment and examine for retransmissions, ICMP errors, or duplicate packets | Network device dropping packets, MTU mismatch, or physical layer issue |
| Slow application despite good bandwidth | Delayed responses even with sufficient throughput | Capture and measure TCP retransmission rates, window sizes, and round-trip times | High retransmission rate indicating packet loss on the path |
| Suspected data exfiltration | Unusual outbound traffic volumes | Capture and analyze destination IPs, protocols, and data volumes | Large encrypted flows to unusual destinations or DNS tunneling patterns |

### Useful Packet Capture Commands

#### Capture Traffic on an Interface

```bash
sudo tcpdump -i eth0
```

#### Capture and Save to a PCAP File

```bash
sudo tcpdump -i eth0 -w capture.pcap
```

#### Read an Existing PCAP

```bash
tcpdump -r capture.pcap
```

#### Open a PCAP in Wireshark

```bash
wireshark capture.pcap
```

These commands form the basic workflow for packet capture and analysis on Linux.

## 16. Real-World Walkthrough: Capturing Cleartext Credentials

> ⚠️ This walkthrough is for educational purposes. Only perform these actions on your own systems or in an authorized lab environment.

**Scenario:** Demonstrating why unencrypted protocols are dangerous on a shared network.

1. Set up a test environment with two machines on the same network segment — one acting as a "victim" running an FTP client, one as the "analyst" running Wireshark.

2. On the analyst machine, start a Wireshark capture on the shared network interface with the display filter:
```
ftp
```

3. On the victim machine, initiate an FTP connection to an FTP server:
```bash
ftp ftp.testserver.local
Username: admin
Password: SecretPass123
```

4. In Wireshark on the analyst machine, the following packets appear immediately:

```
 Packet 1: -> USER admin
 Packet 2: <- 331 Password required for admin.
 Packet 3: -> PASS SecretPass123
 Packet 4: <- 230 User admin logged in.
```

5. The credentials are **completely visible** in plaintext — no decryption required.

6. Right-click any FTP packet → **Follow → TCP Stream** — the entire authentication conversation is visible as readable text.

7. **Lesson:** Replace FTP with SFTP (SSH-based) or FTPS (TLS-based) — encrypted alternatives where the same capture shows only meaningless ciphertext.

## 17. Common Beginner Misconceptions

| ❌ Misconception | ✅ Reality |
|---|---|
| Packet sniffing always requires physical access to network cables | Wireless sniffing in monitor mode requires only being in range — no physical access needed |
| Sniffing on a switched network automatically captures all traffic | Modern switches only forward unicast to intended ports — active techniques like ARP poisoning are required to capture others' traffic |
| Encryption only hides passwords, not other data | TLS encryption hides **all application data** — the full HTTP request, response, and any data transmitted |
| Packet sniffing is always illegal | It is legal and standard practice on networks you own or have explicit permission to monitor — illegality applies to unauthorized sniffing |
| A VPN makes you completely unsniffable | A VPN encrypts traffic between your device and the VPN server — traffic beyond the VPN server, or traffic sniffed before it enters the VPN tunnel, may still be visible |
| Only attackers use packet sniffers | Network engineers, security analysts, and SOC teams use them constantly for legitimate purposes |
| Capturing metadata (IP addresses, timing) is harmless | Metadata alone can reveal browsing habits, communication partners, location, and behavioral patterns — it is never truly "harmless" |
| HTTPS makes all communication private | HTTPS encrypts content, but destination IPs, timing, data volumes, and SNI are still visible in packet captures |
| Packet sniffing automatically modifies traffic | Packet sniffing is normally passive and only observes traffic. Modifying traffic requires additional techniques such as Man-in-the-Middle attacks |

## 18. Common Terminology Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Packet Sniffing** | Intercepting and recording network packets for analysis |
| **Packet Capture** | The act or result of recording network traffic |
| **PCAP** | Packet Capture file format used to store captured traffic |
| **Promiscuous Mode** | NIC mode that captures all received frames, not just those addressed to itself |
| **Monitor Mode** | Wireless NIC mode that captures all wireless frames in range |
| **Passive Sniffing** | Listening to traffic without sending any additional packets |
| **Active Sniffing** | Manipulating network traffic to redirect it toward the sniffer |
| **Port Mirroring / SPAN** | A switch feature copying traffic from source ports to a monitoring port |
| **Network TAP** | A physical device creating a passive copy of traffic for monitoring |
| **Cleartext** | Data transmitted without encryption — fully readable in a capture |
| **Ciphertext** | Encrypted data — unreadable without the decryption key |
| **Credential Capture** | Intercepting authentication data (usernames/passwords) from cleartext protocols |
| **Session Hijacking** | Using a captured session cookie to impersonate an authenticated user |
| **tcpdump** | A command-line packet capture tool |
| **Wireshark** | A GUI packet analyzer (*Chapter 22*) |
| **ARP Poisoning** | Manipulating ARP tables to redirect traffic (*Chapter 25*) |
| **MAC Flooding** | Overwhelming a CAM table to force hub-like broadcast behavior (*Chapter 17*) |
| **SNI** | Server Name Indication — TLS extension revealing target hostname |
| **Beaconing** | Regular, interval-based malware communication visible in captures |
| **Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)** | An attack where sniffing and forwarding creates invisible interception (*Chapter 27*) |
| **Flow** | A sequence of related packets exchanged between endpoints |
| **Payload** | The actual application data carried inside a packet |
| **Frame** | A Layer 2 data unit transmitted across a local network |
| **Capture Filter** | A filter applied before packet capture to reduce recorded traffic |
| **Display Filter** | A filter applied after capture to show only selected packets in Wireshark |

## 19. Common Interview Questions

1. **What is packet sniffing and how does it work?** Intercepting and recording network packets by placing a NIC in promiscuous mode, capturing all frames received on the network segment.
2. **What is the difference between passive and active sniffing?** Passive sniffing just listens to traffic without generating packets; active sniffing sends additional packets to manipulate the network and redirect traffic toward the attacker.
3. **Why doesn't passive sniffing work well on switched networks?** Switches forward unicast frames only to the intended destination port, so passive sniffing only captures your own traffic and broadcasts — not other devices' unicast traffic.
4. **What is promiscuous mode?** A NIC mode where the interface captures all received frames regardless of destination MAC address.
5. **What is the most common technique for sniffing on switched networks?** ARP poisoning — sending forged ARP replies to redirect traffic through the attacker's machine (*Chapter 25*).
6. **What is the best defense against packet sniffing?** Encryption — making captured content unreadable without the decryption key.
7. **What is port mirroring/SPAN and why is it used?** A switch feature copying traffic from monitored ports to a dedicated monitoring port — used by legitimate IDS/IPS and network monitoring systems.
8. **Can HTTPS traffic be sniffed?** The content is encrypted and unreadable; however, metadata (destination IP, timing, data volume, SNI) remains visible.
9. **What is a Network TAP?** A physical device installed inline on a network cable that creates a passive copy of all traffic for monitoring without affecting the live traffic flow.
10. **Why are cleartext protocols dangerous on shared networks?** Credentials and data are transmitted in plain readable text — any device on the same network segment with a packet sniffer can capture them instantly.

## 20. Key Takeaways

### 📋 Summary

- **Packet sniffing** captures network traffic by placing a NIC in promiscuous mode — recording packets for analysis.
- **Passive sniffing** listens without generating traffic; **active sniffing** manipulates the network to redirect traffic.
- Modern switched networks limit passive sniffing — **active techniques like ARP poisoning** are required to intercept others' unicast traffic.
- **Cleartext protocols** (HTTP, FTP, Telnet) expose credentials and data completely — **encryption is the only effective defense**.
- Even encrypted traffic reveals **metadata** (source/destination IPs, timing, volume, SNI) — metadata is never completely innocuous.
- **Port mirroring (SPAN)** and **network TAPs** are how legitimate monitoring infrastructure captures traffic at scale.
- Packet sniffing is a **dual-use capability** — a critical tool for defenders and a powerful weapon for attackers.
