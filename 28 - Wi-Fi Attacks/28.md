## 1. What are Wi-Fi Attacks?

### 📘 Formal Definition

**Wi-Fi attacks** are a class of network attacks that specifically target **IEEE 802.11 wireless networks** (*Chapter 21*), exploiting the unique characteristics of wireless communication — including the open-air transmission medium, the broadcast nature of radio signals, and weaknesses in wireless authentication and encryption protocols — to gain unauthorized access, intercept traffic, or disrupt service.

### 🧠 Simple Explanation

Unlike wired networks where an attacker must physically connect a cable, wireless networks broadcast signals through the air — **anyone within radio range can attempt to interact with them**, without any physical access to the building or infrastructure. This fundamentally expands the attack surface to include the physical space surrounding the network.

### 📡 Real-World Analogy: A Conversation in a Public Space

> Imagine two people having a private conversation in an open park. Unlike a conversation in a soundproofed room (wired network), anyone nearby can listen, and a skilled enough person can even inject themselves into the conversation. The "walls" of a wireless network are radio frequency boundaries — invisible, permeable, and extending well beyond the building itself.

### 🌍 Real-World Examples

- A penetration tester sitting in a client's parking lot and capturing WPA2 handshakes from employees joining the corporate Wi-Fi
- An attacker in a coffee shop creating an Evil Twin access point to intercept all patron traffic
- A security researcher demonstrating WPS PIN cracking against a home router in minutes
- A nation-state actor mapping corporate wireless infrastructure from a vehicle parked outside

## 2. Why Wireless Networks Are Uniquely Vulnerable

### 📡 The Shared, Open Medium

Unlike wired Ethernet, where traffic is confined to cables and switches, wireless signals propagate **omnidirectionally through space**. Any device within range can receive all wireless frames — there are no physical barriers equivalent to a cable's physical isolation.

### 🔑 Authentication Before Association

Connecting to a wireless network involves authentication — but the authentication mechanism itself (WPA2 PSK, WPA3 SAE, WEP) can be targeted, unlike wired networks where physical cable access provides inherent "authentication."

### 🏙️ Extended Physical Attack Surface

An attacker doesn't need to be inside a building. With a directional antenna, attacks can be launched from **hundreds of meters away** — parking lots, adjacent buildings, or nearby public spaces.

### 📶 Signal Characteristics

Radio signals:
- Pass through walls, floors, and ceilings (especially 2.4 GHz)
- Extend beyond intended coverage areas
- Are receivable by any compatible radio hardware
- Cannot be easily confined to a specific physical perimeter

### 📡 Radio Signals Cannot Be Directed Perfectly

Even when an access point is placed inside a building, radio waves often extend beyond walls, windows, and floors.

This means attackers may be able to perform reconnaissance or attacks from:

- Parking lots
- Adjacent buildings
- Public sidewalks
- Nearby cafés

This practice is commonly known as **war driving** when performed from a moving vehicle.

## 3. Wireless Reconnaissance — Passive and Active

Before launching any wireless attack, an attacker surveys the available wireless landscape.

### 🎯 Passive Reconnaissance

Setting a wireless interface to **monitor mode** (*Chapter 21, Section 7*) allows capture of all wireless management frames in range without associating to any network.

Information revealed:
- **SSIDs** — network names (including "hidden" networks, revealed in probe requests)
- **BSSIDs** — MAC addresses of access points (*Chapter 4*)
- **Channel** — frequency being used (*Chapter 21, Section 12*)
- **Security type** — WPA2, WPA3, OPN (open)
- **Signal strength** — RSSI values for AP localization
- **Connected clients** — devices currently associated to each AP

```bash
# Enable monitor mode:
airmon-ng start wlan0

# Passive scan of all wireless traffic:
airodump-ng wlan0mon
```

```
 BSSID              PWR  Beacons  #Data  CH  ENC   ESSID
 AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF  -62      120    450   6  WPA2  CorpNetwork
 11:22:33:44:55:66  -78       80    120  11  WPA2  HomeWiFi
 GG:HH:II:JJ:KK:LL -45      200   1200   1  OPN   CoffeeShop_Guest
```

### 🎯 Active Reconnaissance

Sending probe requests or association attempts to gather additional information — more detectable but can reveal information not visible passively.

## 4. Evil Twin / Rogue Access Point Attacks

### 📘 Formal Definition

An **Evil Twin attack** creates a fraudulent wireless access point that mimics a legitimate network by broadcasting the same SSID — tricking clients into associating with the attacker's AP instead of the real one.

### 🧠 How It Works

```
 Legitimate AP:
 SSID: CorpNetwork | BSSID: AA:BB:CC | CH: 6 | WPA2

 Evil Twin AP (attacker):
 SSID: CorpNetwork | BSSID: EE:EE:EE | CH: 6 | OPN or WPA2
        ↑ Same SSID                            ↑ Often open (no password)
```

1. Attacker creates an AP with the **identical SSID** as the target network
2. Attacker optionally sends **deauthentication frames** (*Section 5*) to disconnect clients from the real AP
3. Disconnected clients automatically search for and reconnect to available networks broadcasting the known SSID
4. Clients reconnect to the **stronger-signal Evil Twin** — often the attacker is physically closer
5. Clients send all traffic through the attacker's AP, enabling **full MITM positioning** (*Chapter 27*)

### 🧠 Why Clients Connect Automatically

Devices remember previously connected networks and connect automatically when the SSID is seen again. The device has no reliable way to verify which physical AP it is connecting to — it trusts the SSID.

### 🛡️ Defense

- **WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise (802.1X)** — clients can verify the RADIUS server certificate, detecting an Evil Twin that can't present valid credentials
- **Wireless Intrusion Detection Systems (WIDS)** — monitor for duplicate SSIDs with different BSSIDs
- **User awareness** — avoiding automatic reconnection to networks in unfamiliar locations

## 5. Deauthentication (Deauth) Attacks

### 📘 Formal Definition

A **deauthentication attack** exploits the 802.11 management frame protocol by sending **forged deauthentication frames** to a wireless client or access point, forcing targeted devices to disconnect from the network.

### 🧠 How It Works

In the 802.11 protocol, **management frames** (including deauthentication frames) were originally **not authenticated or encrypted** — any device can send a deauthentication frame claiming to be any other device, and the receiving device will comply.

```
 Normal operation:
 [ Client ] <──────────────> [ AP ]

 Deauth Attack:
 [ Attacker ] --> Deauth frame (spoofed as AP) --> [ Client ]
 "You have been deauthenticated from the network"

 Client disconnects immediately, then attempts to reconnect
```

### 🎯 Why Attackers Use Deauth

- **Force WPA2 handshake capture:** Disconnecting a client forces a fresh association — the WPA2 four-way handshake (*Section 6*) is transmitted during reconnection, which the attacker captures
- **Evil Twin enablement:** Disrupting the legitimate AP forces clients onto the attacker's Evil Twin
- **Denial of Service:** Continuously deauthenticating clients disrupts network availability

```bash
# Send deauth frames to all clients on a target AP:
aireplay-ng --deauth 10 -a AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF wlan0mon
          ↑ 10 deauth frames  ↑ Target AP BSSID
```

### 🛡️ Defense: 802.11w (Management Frame Protection)

**IEEE 802.11w** adds **cryptographic protection to management frames**, making it impossible to forge deauthentication frames without knowing the session key. WPA3 mandates 802.11w — WPA2 supports it as an optional extension.

## 6. WPA2 Handshake Capture and Offline Cracking

### 📘 The WPA2 Four-Way Handshake

When a client joins a WPA2-Personal network, a **four-way handshake** is exchanged between the client and AP. This handshake:
- Verifies both parties know the Pre-Shared Key (PSK)
- Establishes the session encryption key
- Critically: contains enough information for **offline cracking** if captured

```
 Client                              AP
    |                                 |
    | <---- ANonce (random) --------- |   (AP sends a random number)
    |                                 |
    | ----- MIC + SNonce -----------> |   (Client proves knowledge of PSK)
    |                                 |
    | <---- MIC + Group Key --------- |   (AP proves knowledge of PSK)
    |                                 |
    | ----- ACK ----------------------|   (Handshake complete)
```

### 🎯 The Attack

1. Attacker captures the four-way handshake (by monitoring during natural reconnection or by forcing reconnection via deauth)
2. The handshake is saved to a PCAP file — **no active connection needed from this point**
3. Attacker performs **offline dictionary/brute-force attack** — comparing millions of candidate passwords to what the handshake should produce if the password were correct

```bash
# Step 1: Capture handshake (targeted at specific AP):
airodump-ng -c 6 --bssid AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -w capture wlan0mon

# Step 2: Force reconnection via deauth:
aireplay-ng --deauth 5 -a AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF wlan0mon

# Step 3: Offline cracking with a wordlist:
aircrack-ng capture.cap -w /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt
```

### 🧠 Why This Works

The PSK is never directly transmitted — but its **derivative** (used in the MIC) is. An attacker with the handshake can test candidate passwords locally at extremely high speed using GPUs.

```
 Testing "password123":
 Compute expected MIC using "password123" as PSK
 Compare to captured MIC
 Match? -> Password found!
 No match? -> Try next password
 (Millions of tests per second with GPU acceleration)
```

### 🛡️ Defense

- **Strong, random passphrase** — long, random passwords that aren't in wordlists
- **WPA3** — SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals) prevents offline dictionary attacks
- **WPA2-Enterprise** — individual certificates, no shared PSK to crack

## 7. PMKID Attack

### 📘 Formal Definition

The **PMKID attack** (discovered 2018) allows WPA2-Personal cracking **without capturing a four-way handshake** — requiring only a single frame from the AP.

### 🧠 How It Works

The **PMKID (Pairwise Master Key Identifier)** is a value derived from the network's PSK and is present in the first frame of the WPA2 association process — transmitted by the AP to every connecting client.

```
 PMKID = HMAC-SHA1(PMK, "PMK Name" || AP_MAC || Client_MAC)
 where PMK is derived from the PSK
```

An attacker can request this frame from any AP **without associating** — then perform the same offline dictionary attack used against captured handshakes.

```bash
# Capture PMKID (using hcxdumptool):
hcxdumptool -i wlan0mon -o output.pcapng --enable_status=1

# Convert and crack:
hcxpcapngtool -o hash.hc22000 output.pcapng
hashcat -m 22000 hash.hc22000 /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt
```

> 🔑 The PMKID attack is more dangerous than handshake capture because it requires **no connected clients and no deauth attack** — a single AP response is enough to begin cracking offline.

## 8. WEP Cracking

### 📘 Why WEP Is Completely Broken

**WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)** (*Chapter 21, Section 9*) uses the RC4 stream cipher with a flawed implementation that reuses initialization vectors (IVs). By capturing enough traffic, statistical analysis of IV collisions allows the key to be recovered — **regardless of key length**.

```bash
# Capture enough WEP IVs:
airodump-ng -c 6 --bssid AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -w wep_capture wlan0mon

# Inject traffic to accelerate IV collection:
aireplay-ng -3 -b AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF wlan0mon

# Crack the WEP key:
aircrack-ng wep_capture.cap
```

**Time to crack:** Typically **under 60 seconds** on a network with sufficient traffic — completely and thoroughly broken.

> 🔑 **No WEP-protected network should be considered secure under any circumstances.** Any network still using WEP is immediately and completely vulnerable. This is not a theoretical concern — it is a practical reality achievable by anyone with basic tools and a few minutes.

## 9. WPA2-Enterprise Attacks

**WPA2-Enterprise** uses 802.1X with a RADIUS server for authentication (*Chapter 21, Section 11*) — more secure than Personal/PSK, but not immune to attack.

### 🎯 Rogue RADIUS / PEAP Downgrade Attack

When a client connects using PEAP (a common EAP method) — the inner authentication (username/password) is protected by TLS. If the client doesn't validate the RADIUS server certificate, an Evil Twin with a rogue RADIUS server can:

1. Accept the client's connection
2. Present any self-signed certificate
3. If the client doesn't verify the certificate and connects anyway → the attacker captures the MSCHAP credential exchange
4. Offline cracking of the NTLM hash can recover the user's domain password

```bash
# hostapd-wpe creates an Evil Twin with a rogue RADIUS server:
hostapd-wpe /etc/hostapd-wpe/hostapd-wpe.conf
# Captures: identity, challenge, response -> offline cracking
```

### 🛡️ Defense

- Configure EAP clients to **verify the RADIUS server certificate**
- Deploy **certificate pinning** for the RADIUS server cert
- Use **EAP-TLS** (mutual certificate authentication) instead of PEAP-MSCHAPv2

## 10. WPS Attacks

**WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup)** is a feature designed to simplify adding devices to a WPA2 network using a PIN — but contains a critical design flaw.

### 🎯 WPS PIN Brute Force

The 8-digit WPS PIN is checked in **two halves independently** — the first 4 digits, then the last 4 (with the last digit being a checksum). This means instead of 100,000,000 possible combinations, there are effectively only **11,000** to try.

```bash
# WPS PIN attack using Reaver:
reaver -i wlan0mon -b AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -vv
# Recovers WPS PIN and then the WPA2 PSK within minutes to hours
```

### 🎯 Pixie Dust Attack

A more sophisticated offline variant that exploits weak random number generation in some WPS implementations — recovering the PIN **almost instantly** against vulnerable routers.

```bash
reaver -i wlan0mon -b AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -K 1  # -K 1 enables Pixie Dust
```

### 🛡️ Defense

- **Disable WPS entirely** on all access points — it provides marginal convenience benefit for significant security risk
- Some routers implement WPS lockout after failed attempts — verify this is enabled if WPS cannot be disabled

## 11. Karma Attack

### 📘 Formal Definition

The **Karma attack** exploits the behavior of wireless clients that **broadcast probe requests** for previously connected networks — advertising the SSIDs of networks they're looking for.

### 🧠 How It Works

```
 Phone (not connected):
 "Is 'HomeWiFi' here? Is 'CoffeeShop_WiFi' here? Is 'CorpNetwork' here?"
 (Probe requests for remembered networks)

 Karma Attack AP:
 "Yes! I am all of those networks!" (responds to every probe request)
 Client automatically connects to attacker's AP
```

Modern operating systems have partially mitigated Karma by using **MAC randomization during probing** (*Chapter 21, Section 8*) and limiting which networks they probe for. However, older devices and some configurations remain vulnerable.

> 🔑 The Karma attack is the technical basis behind the common advice to **forget networks you no longer use** and **disable auto-connect to open networks** — every remembered network SSID your device broadcasts is a potential Karma attack target.

## 12. Wi-Fi Attacks and MITM Positioning

Most Wi-Fi attacks are used to establish a **MITM position** (*Chapter 27*) from which further exploitation occurs:

```
 Wi-Fi Attack Chain:
 ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │ 1. Reconnaissance (airodump-ng) — map the target     │
 │ 2. Deauth Attack — disconnect client from real AP    │
 │ 3. Evil Twin — client connects to attacker's AP      │
 │ 4. MITM Established — all client traffic intercepted │
 │ 5. SSL Stripping — downgrade HTTPS to HTTP           │
 │ 6. Credential Capture — harvest credentials          │
 └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

This end-to-end chain demonstrates how individual Wi-Fi attack techniques combine into a complete attack workflow.

## 14. How This Works in Real Life

| Environment | Wi-Fi Attacks in Practice |
|---|---|
| **Home Networks** | WPS is commonly enabled and vulnerable; weak PSK passphrases are a primary target; consumer APs rarely implement 802.11w or WPA3 |
| **Enterprise Networks** | WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise with 802.1X should be standard; WIDS deployed to detect rogue APs and deauth attacks; VLANs separate guest and corporate traffic |
| **Public Wi-Fi** | Extremely high-risk environment — Evil Twin and Karma attacks are practical and common; treat all public Wi-Fi as hostile |
| **Penetration Testing** | Wireless testing is a standard component of physical and external assessments — wardriving, Evil Twin, handshake capture, and Enterprise credential attacks are routinely performed |
| **SOC** | WIDS alerts for rogue APs, deauth floods, and PMKID attempts are high-priority — indicating active wireless reconnaissance or attack |

### Industries That Rely on Wireless Security

Wireless security is especially important in:

- Corporate offices
- Universities
- Hospitals
- Airports
- Hotels
- Retail stores
- Manufacturing facilities
- Government organizations

Any environment with large numbers of wireless devices benefits from strong Wi-Fi security controls.

| Attack | Primary Defense |
|---|---|
| **Evil Twin** | WPA2/WPA3-Enterprise with RADIUS cert validation; WIDS monitoring |
| **Deauth Attack** | 802.11w (Management Frame Protection); WPA3 (mandatory 802.11w) |

| **PMKID Attack** | Long, random passphrases; WPA3 |
| **WEP Cracking** | **Stop using WEP immediately** — upgrade to WPA2 or WPA3 |
| **WPS Attacks** | Disable WPS entirely |
| **Karma Attack** | Forget unused networks; disable auto-connect on open networks; MAC randomization |
| **Enterprise (PEAP/MSCHAP)** | Validate RADIUS server certificate; use EAP-TLS instead of PEAP |
| **All Wi-Fi Attacks** | Use a **VPN** on untrusted networks — encrypts all traffic regardless of Wi-Fi security |

## 17. Real-World Walkthrough: WPA2 Handshake Capture and Cracking

> ⚠️ For educational purposes only. Only perform on networks you own or have explicit written authorization to test.

**Environment:**
- Target AP: SSID `TestNetwork`, BSSID `AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF`, Channel 6
- Attacker: Kali Linux, wireless card supporting monitor mode (`wlan0`)

**Step 1 — Enable monitor mode:**
```bash
airmon-ng check kill        # Kill processes interfering with monitor mode
airmon-ng start wlan0       # Enable monitor mode -> wlan0mon
```

**Step 2 — Identify target AP and connected clients:**
```bash
airodump-ng wlan0mon
# Note: Target AP on CH 6, BSSID AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF
# Note: Client connected: BB:CC:DD:EE:FF:GG
```

**Step 3 — Focus capture on target AP:**
```bash
airodump-ng -c 6 --bssid AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -w handshake_capture wlan0mon
# Watching for WPA handshake in top-right corner...
```

**Step 4 — Force reconnection via deauthentication:**
```bash
# Open new terminal:
aireplay-ng --deauth 5 -a AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF -c BB:CC:DD:EE:FF:GG wlan0mon
# Sends 5 deauth frames to the specific client
```

**Step 5 — Verify handshake captured:**
```
 CH 6 ][ Elapsed: 30 s ][ WPA handshake: AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF
```

**Step 6 — Offline cracking with Aircrack-ng:**
```bash
aircrack-ng handshake_capture.cap -w /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt
```
```
 KEY FOUND! [ Password123! ]
 Master Key: A1 B2 C3 D4 ...
```

**Lesson:** The password `Password123!` — found in the rockyou.txt wordlist — was cracked in seconds. A password like `x7K#mQ2$pL9vR4nW` is not in any wordlist and would require years of brute force — effectively secure against this attack.

### What You Should Observe

During the lab, identify:

- The target SSID
- The target BSSID
- The operating channel
- The captured WPA handshake
- The deauthentication frames
- The captured PCAP file
- The successful password recovery (if using a weak password)

Being able to recognize these artifacts is an essential skill for wireless penetration testers, incident responders, and SOC analysts.

## 18. Common Beginner Misconceptions

| ❌ Misconception | ✅ Reality |
|---|---|
| WPA2 is unbreakable | WPA2-Personal is vulnerable to offline dictionary attacks if a weak passphrase is used — the handshake can be captured without the attacker knowing the password first |
| Hidden SSIDs provide meaningful security | Hidden SSIDs are trivially revealed in probe requests from connected clients — they provide essentially no security benefit |
| More Wi-Fi signal bars means the connection is secure | Signal strength says nothing about security — a strong signal from an Evil Twin is equally or more dangerous than a weak legitimate signal |
| WPA3 completely eliminates all Wi-Fi attack risk | WPA3 eliminates offline PSK cracking and mandates 802.11w — but other attacks (Evil Twin with rogue RADIUS for Enterprise, social engineering, etc.) remain viable |
| WPS with a strong PIN is safe | The WPS protocol's design flaw (split PIN checking) applies regardless of PIN strength — disable WPS |
| Public Wi-Fi with a password is secure | The password used is shared with everyone — any other patron can decrypt your traffic; use a VPN on all public Wi-Fi |
| Changing the SSID to something obscure prevents targeting | Obscure SSIDs still appear in airodump-ng scans and are fully targetable |
| 5 GHz Wi-Fi is more secure than 2.4 GHz | The security of a network is determined by its authentication protocol (WPA2/WPA3), not its frequency band |
| MAC address filtering makes a Wi-Fi network secure | MAC addresses are easily observed and can be spoofed, so MAC filtering should never be relied upon as a primary security control |

## 20. Common Interview Questions

1. **What is an Evil Twin attack?** Creating a rogue AP with the same SSID as a legitimate network to trick clients into connecting through the attacker's device.
2. **How does a deauthentication attack work?** Sending forged 802.11 deauthentication frames (which are unauthenticated by default) to force clients to disconnect from their AP.
3. **What is 802.11w and why was it introduced?** Management Frame Protection — adds cryptographic authentication to management frames to prevent forged deauth and disassociation attacks.
4. **Why is WPA2-Personal vulnerable to offline cracking?** The four-way handshake can be captured and subjected to offline dictionary/brute-force attacks — no online access is needed after capture.
5. **What is the PMKID attack and why is it more efficient than handshake capture?** It obtains a crackable value from a single AP frame without needing a connected client or deauth attack.
6. **Why is WEP completely insecure?** Its RC4 implementation reuses initialization vectors — statistical analysis of captured traffic recovers the key regardless of length, typically in under a minute.
7. **What is the WPS design flaw?** The 8-digit PIN is checked in two independent halves, reducing the effective search space from 100 million to about 11,000 combinations.
8. **What is a Karma attack?** Responding to client probe requests for any SSID — causing devices to automatically connect to the attacker's AP thinking it's a remembered network.
9. **What is the most effective defense against WPA2 handshake cracking?** A long, randomly generated passphrase not found in any wordlist — or upgrading to WPA3 which prevents offline cracking entirely.
10. **Why should WPS always be disabled?** Its structural design flaw makes it vulnerable to brute-force PIN recovery regardless of the underlying WPA2 passphrase strength.

## 21. Key Takeaways

### 📋 Summary

- Wireless networks are uniquely vulnerable because radio signals propagate through space — any device within range can interact with them without physical access.
- **Evil Twin** attacks create fraudulent APs with legitimate SSIDs to establish MITM positions.
- **Deauthentication attacks** exploit unauthenticated 802.11 management frames — defended by **802.11w/WPA3**.
- **WPA2 handshake capture** enables offline cracking — effectiveness depends entirely on passphrase strength.
- The **PMKID attack** is more efficient, requiring only a single AP frame and no connected clients.
- **WEP is completely broken** — no network should still be using it.
- **WPS has a critical design flaw** — should always be disabled.
- The **Karma attack** exploits automatic network reconnection behavior.
- **WPA3 with SAE** prevents offline cracking and mandates 802.11w — the current recommended standard.
